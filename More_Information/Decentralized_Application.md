Here is a more in-depth explanation of each of the decentralized applications selected for LLM implementation.

- **Tokenization:** A blockchain is, in its essence, a decentralized timestamp authority, in which users are not necessarily required to trust one another. In contrast, conventional centralized timestamp authorities—such as governmental notary offices—rely on public officials to consolidate the current status of citizens and some high-value assets. These institutions maintain records, for example, of real estate ownership, an area particularly suited for decentralization through blockchain technologies. Hence, this application was selected as a representative example of asset tokenization usage in our study.

    For this use case our prompt instructed LLMs to implement the *RealEstateNotary* contract. Its functionalities should enable government officials to mint tokens representing real estate and assign them to property owners, which, in turn, may then freely transfer their tokens. This architecture should be able to eliminate the necessity of a traditional centralized body responsible for managing such transfers. Each token should be associated with a URI that would, in a real scenario, contain metadata about the property, including its address, usage, dimensions, height, and other relevant details. Users should be permitted to request changes to this metadata by submitting a new URI for review, which government employees retain the authority to approve or reject.

- **Financial Services:** A common type of financial product—frequently available in Brazil—are financial pools, or *consórcios*, in Portuguese. These enable multiple users to collaboratively finance high-value goods, such as real estate or vehicles, by paying regular monthly installments. Once the pooled amount is reached, one participant is selected at random to receive the funds required to purchase the good. The selection winner, however, remains obligated to continue paying installments until all participants have also acquired the item. Although these arrangements are typically managed by financial institutions, they can be implemented using smart contracts on Ethereum, thereby removing the need for intermediaries.
  
    For this application, we prompted LLMs to develop the *FinancialPoolManager* contract. It should allow users to establish their own pools by defining parameters such as the desired good, its price, number of installments, and the minimum required number of participants. Upon meeting the minimum participant threshold, the pool owner may activate the pool, which automatically calculates installment amounts and tracks due payments. Each time a payment is successfully made, the contract automatically checks whether the target value has been achieved; if so, it should randomly select an eligible participant to receive the funds. Once all participants have received their allocated share, the pool should be marked as completed.

- **Digital Marketplace:** Digital marketplaces serve as on-chain platforms where blockchain users can acquire tokens, which can represent digital or real-life goods. When these applications involve real-world items, they are typically integrated with IoT-based supply chain infrastructures. While sensors are often employed to track goods throughout their life-cycles, other functionalities, such as purchase authorization, may also be implemented through blockchain technology. This is particularly relevant in the context of medicine prescriptions, where doctors issue prescriptions that pharmacies require for legally selling controlled drugs. Smart contracts could facilitate this process by eliminating the need for intermediaries who verify prescriptions, incorporating four key components: governmental authorization for hospitals and drug manufacturers, doctor-issued prescriptions, hospital-based purchase authorization, and pharmaceutical production.

    For this scenario, we created a prompt instructing LLMs to implement a complete decentralized application consisting of four contracts: *AuthorizationCertificates*, *HospitalManagement*, *Prescriptions*, and *PharmaCompany*. The first is government-controlled, authorizing hospitals and manufacturers to operate. The second and third represent a simplified hospital ecosystem in which doctors can issue prescriptions to patients and authorize drug sales through their automatic validation. Finally, the last implements an example of a pharmaceutical company that directly sells its stock to end users, without the need for the intermediary. This ecosystem should enable patients to purchase directly from manufacturers, ensuring that each prescription is used only once for the correct medicine and dose.

- **Decentralized Decision Making:** In blockchain environments, tokens could also be used to determine user voting power in group decision-making processes. As previously illustrated with the tokenization example, real estate ownership can be represented via tokens, which naturally extends their utility to scenarios such as condominium assemblies. From this reasoning, each apartment owner is thus assigned a token representing their voting power, which may be cast within voting pools related to condo decisions.

    In this context, we created a prompt instructing LLMs to implement the *CondoVotingSystem* contract. This system allows token holders to submit proposals that are subject to community votes. Voting power should be locked for a specific pool upon initiation of the vote and cannot be exercised by the contract owner within these pools. The owner may define standard voting durations and minimum quorum requirements for early termination of the voting period. While a pool remains active, voters should be permitted to change their votes freely.

- **(Bonus) Exchange Wallet:** Within blockchain environments that support currency, such as Ethereum, wallet contracts serve as common applications allowing users to deposit funds. This functionality aligns closely with the concept of bridges—off-chain infrastructures, which are designed to enable users to exchange one cryptocurrency for another across different chains. A bridge operates through a contract on one chain that collects specific amounts of funds along with target addresses and currencies, and emits an event (e.g., on Ethereum) visible off-chain. Off-chain infrastructure then interprets the event and activates a corresponding contract owned by the bridge operator on the destination chain to send the exchanged currency to the target address. Note that such structure can be understood as two wallets, one in each chain.

    For this application, our prompt instructed LLMs to implement several contracts: *Wallet*, *Oracle*, and *Exchange*. The first is a standard wallet allowing users to deposit funds, transfer them to other accounts, and authorize third parties to conduct transactions on their behalf. It also serves as the interface for the service owner to interact with oracle and exchange contracts that facilitate bridge operations. Users may choose a supported currency, which can then be acquired by invoking the Exchange contract from within the Wallet, provided that recent enough conversion rate data is available. We did not implement any off-chain infrastructure; instead, we only developed up to the point of generating an event in Ethereum containing information about the target currency, address, and value. Furthermore, on-chain oracles are not strictly required for bridge operations that may utilize them directly off-chain, as they present several challenges. Nevertheless, a simplified oracle was implemented to enrich our test environment with additional practical application context.
